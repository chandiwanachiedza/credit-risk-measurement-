The Lufthansa Group is a global aviation group made up of Network Airlines, Eurowings and
Aviation Services. Its main business is international passenger air transport, focusing on premium
airlines such as Lufthansa, SWISS, Austrian, Brussels Airlines, and ITA Airways.
The aviation industry remains highly sensitive to economic and external shocks, and in 2020 the
industry faced several challenges due to the Covid-19 pandemic. There was a global shutdown, and
governments globally had travel restrictions which squeezed several airlines income, yet they had
large fixed costs. Lufthansa was very close to insolvency, reporting a €2.1 billion loss for the first
quarter (Amaro, 2020). The firm's scale and impact on Germany’s economy led to government
intervention, resulting in a €9 billion bailout to avert systemic fallout (Geh, S. 2023).
By applying different credit risk models, the study aims to assess whether these models
were able to capture the firm’s underlying financial vulnerability before the crisis materialised.
Altman’s Z-Score
The Altman’s Z-score is used to predict the probability of a company defaulting using a combination
of five financial ratios. The analysis uses the original Altman Z-score model for publicly listed firms,
which combines five accounting-based ratios covering liquidity, profitability, leverage, solvency, and
asset turnover to estimate financial distress risk. A score below 1.8 indicates high distress, distress
zone, 1.8–3.0 is the grey zone, and above 3.0 indicates strong financial health, safe zone. Lufthansa
had a z-score of 1.83 in 2017 indicating it was in the grey zone and 1.37 in 2019 indicating it was in
the distress zone. The score provides an indication that Lufthansa had increased financial distress risk;
however, despite its accuracy the score is a static, backward-looking snapshot based on historical
financial statements, failing to account for recent operational shifts. It ignores external factors
including management and macroeconomic events and may not always be as effective. For example,
Shi, Y and Li, X (2023) found that Altman’s Z-score model failed to predict the collapse of airlines,
like Thomas Cook and VIM Airlines, during the Covid-19 crisis because their bankruptcies were
driven by non-financial issues, such as board mismanagement, or legal disputes rather than purely
poor financial ratios. They also concluded that the Z”
-score is a highly promising predictive model for
the aviation sector as it is more robust. However, even then its results must be interpreted alongside
qualitative factors like management quality and government support.
The Ohlson O-score is an accounting-based bankruptcy prediction model that estimates the
probability that a company will experience financial distress or bankruptcy by using information from
its financial statements.
The Ohlson O-score results indicate that Lufthansa’s financial risk increased between 2017 and 2019.
The O-score rose from -1.89 in 2017 to -1.13 in 2019, while the implied probability of default
increased from 13% to 24%. Although the company was not classified as being in immediate financial
distress, as both probabilities are below 50%, the upward movement in both indicators suggests that
Lufthansa’s financial condition weakened before 2020.
The Merton structural model remains one of the most influential and widely applied frameworks in
both academic research and industry practice for credit risk measurement. The model treats a firm's
equity as a European call option on its underlying assets, with the strike price equal to the face value
of the firm's debt maturing at time T. When the asset value falls below the debt obligation at maturity,
the firm defaults. We use the yields on long-term government bonds to model the risk free rate. For
Lufthansa, we use the German 10-year Bond yields for 2017 and 2019, which were 0.283% and
approximately -0.25%, respectively (Macrotrends, 1956-2026). The debt threshold was estimated as
the sum of all short-term debt plus half of long-term debt and asset volatility was initially
approximated using the volatility of equity returns and then refined iteratively. The process involved
repeatedly estimating asset values, calculating implied asset returns, and updating the volatility
estimate until convergence was reached.
In 2017, the estimated asset value was €8.6 compared with a debt threshold of €3.7 billion. The
corresponding distance to default was 7.49. This produced an implied probability of default that was
effectively zero.
In 2019, the estimated asset value increased substantially to €16.9 billion while the debt threshold rose
to €5.8 billion. Although the distance to default fell slightly to 6.28, the implied probability of default
remained effectively zero.
The Merton model underestimated Lufthansa’s eventual distress risk, whereas, in reality Lufthansa
was exposed to a sudden and severe external shock during the COVID-19 pandemic, which sharply
reduced revenues, liquidity, and financing capacity. As a result, the firm entered distress despite
appearing financially stable under the Merton framework. Although Merton model is typically the
more sophisticated model, default in the Merton framework occurs only when asset value falls below
debt at maturity. However, Lufthansa's near insolvency in 2020 was driven by a liquidity crisis. The
firm had positive asset value but insufficient cash to meet immediate obligations. The model does not
distinguish between insolvency and illiquidity defaults. The collapse of Long-Term Capital
Management (LTCM) in 1998 also demonstrated the liquidity flaw of the Merton model. Majumder,
D (2006) found that a firm can be fundamentally solvent according to the Merton model's asset-to-
debt ratios yet still fail because the model fails to distinguish between insolvency and illiquidity,
ignoring the systemic risks and market sentiment that can drain a firm's cash long before its debt
reaches maturity.
